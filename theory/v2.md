## 4. Meaning Formation Model (SRCM)

This section describes how meaning is formed in human communication. The goal is not to propose a solution, but to clarify **where meaning is generated and distorted.**

### 4.1 When Meaning Emerges
People do not share words. They share **triggers**.

Each person holds a unique internal image of a word. This image is formed through accumulated experience: past success and failure, professional context, responsibility, and role. Words themselves do not contain meaning; they activate it.

Meaning is not transmitted. **Meaning is generated.**
- Words are triggers
- Meaning is not transmitted
- Meaning is generated

### 4.2 Core Relation

Meaning does not emerge from language alone.
It emerges from the interaction between three elements:

- **S — Speaker Image**  
  The internal meaning the speaker associates with a word.

- **R — Receiver Image**  
  The receiver’s internal representation, shaped by experience and expertise.

- **C — Context / Constraint**  
  Structural forces such as authority, evaluation pressure, risk, time, and physical limits.

This relationship is expressed as:

$$M = f(S, R, C)$$

However, this does **not** mean that S, R, and C contribute equally.

In practice, **Context ($C$) intervenes twice**:
- It shapes how the message is interpreted.
- It determines whether interpretation can turn into action.

Meaning ($M$) is therefore not what is said,
nor what is understood,
but what survives constraint.

### 4.3 Speaker Image (S)
The speaker holds a personal internal image of a word. This represents what the speaker *means*, not what is explicitly stated.
- It is often implicit.
- It is shaped by experience and intent.

### 4.4 Receiver Image (R)
The receiver interprets a word by referencing their own internal image (prior experience, expertise, and mental models). This interpretation is rarely dictionary-based.

### 4.5 Context / Constraint (C)
Interpretation always passes through contextual constraints, which act as filters on meaning:
- Authority and hierarchy
- Evaluation pressure
- Fear, hesitation, and expectation
- Organizational culture, role, and accountability
- **Physical and structural constraints (e.g., budget, time, safety standards, technical limits)**

These factors can amplify, distort, or override both $S$ and $R$.

### 4.6 Meaning Generation (M)
The meaning that actually drives action is not $S$ or $R$, but the meaning that emerges **after passing through context ($C$)**. This generated meaning ($M$) determines behavior: compliance, resistance, avoidance, or superficial agreement.

### 4.7 Relation to Judgment Structure
These categories describe outcomes, not causes.
The judgment types identified in this project are **outcomes of the meaning-formation process**, not just UI categories:

- **S / R Mismatch (Issues of interpretation)**
  - A: Reference Missing
  - B: Reference-Resolved
  - C: Immediate Comprehension
- **C Dominant (Issues of structural distortion)**
  - E: Authority Override
  - F: Explanation Avoidance
  - G: Surface Compliance
- **M Collapse (Issues of system failure)**
  - D: Structural Mismatch

This reframes the taxonomy from a list of "UI problems" to a **map of meaning-formation failure modes.**

### 4.8 Implications
In practice, **contextual constraints often dominate meaning formation.** UI can primarily address mismatches between $S$ and $R$. Once $C$ becomes dominant, meaning formation moves outside the reach of UI.

## 5. Constraint-Weighted Meaning

In some environments, **Context / Constraint ($C$)** dominates meaning formation.

Meaning is not equally influenced by all variables.
In practice, constraints often outweigh intent and understanding.

This can be expressed as a weighted function:

$$
M = w_c \cdot C + (1 - w_c) \cdot f(S, R)
$$

Where:

- **$w_c$ (Weight of Constraint):**  
  The degree to which structural constraints dominate meaning formation.
  - $w_c \to 1$ : Meaning is dictated almost entirely by constraint.
  - $w_c \to 0$ : Meaning is formed primarily through $S$ and $R$.

### 5.1 Examples of High Constraint Weight ($w_c \to 1$)

These cases illustrate why judgment can stall or collapse
*even when intent and understanding are clear.*

---

**Example 1: Authority-Dominated Organizations**

- The instruction is clear.
- Everyone understands what should be done.
- Past experience shows that independent action is punished.

**Result:**  
Meaning collapses into compliance or inaction.  
This corresponds to **Type E (Authority Override)**.

---

**Example 2: Structural Impossibility**

- The goal is understood and agreed upon.
- Required resources (time, budget, tools) do not exist.
- Acting would guarantee failure.

**Result:**  
Meaning cannot translate into action.  
This corresponds to **Type D (Structural Mismatch)**.

---

**Example 3: Responsibility Deadlock**

- The task is clear.
- Execution risk is high.
- Responsibility for failure is undefined.

**Result:**  
No one moves, despite shared understanding.  
This often appears as **Type G (Surface Compliance)** or **Type F (Explanation Avoidance)**.

---

### 5.2 Implication

When $w_c$ becomes dominant, **improving language or UI has no effect**.

The problem is not misunderstanding.
The problem is that **meaning is structurally prevented from becoming action**.

This explains why some judgment failures feel inevitable,
and why they persist even after repeated clarification.

---

### 5.3 Relation to Judgment Types

High constraint weight explains several judgment failure types observed in V1:

- **Type E — Authority Override**  
  Judgment exists, but is overwritten by power structure  
  ($w_c \to 1$)

- **Type F — Explanation Avoidance**  
  Explanation is suppressed because it is structurally unsafe  
  (High $w_c$ + emotional risk)

- **Type G — Surface Compliance**  
  Apparent agreement without ownership of judgment  
  (Action minimized under constraint)

These are not communication failures.
They are **constraint-dominant meaning formations**.

---

### 5.4 Implication

When $w_c$ is high:

- Better wording does not help
- Better prompts do not help
- Better UI does not help

This is not a design limitation.
It is a **structural boundary**.

Judgment Structure does not attempt to lower $w_c$.
It makes its presence visible.

## 6. Recursive Constraint: Interpretation and Action

Why does a high $w_c$ (Section 5) completely stop action? 
Because structurally, **Constraint ($C$) strikes twice.**

The function $f$ is not a single step. It is a composite of **Interpretation ($g$)** and **Action ($h$)**.

### The Precise Formula
$$M = h(g(S, C), C)$$

### Step 1: Interpretation ($g$)
$$R = g(S, C)$$
First, the constraint filters how the message is *understood*.
- **Input:** $S$ (Signal) + $C$ (Bias, Culture, Fear)
- **Output:** $R$ (Internal Representation)
* *Failure Mode:* The receiver understands the words, but the *intent* is warped by the context (e.g., "He said 'free', but in this company ($C$), that means 'cheap'.").

### Step 2: Action ($h$)
$$M = h(R, C)$$
Next, the constraint filters whether the understood meaning can be *executed*.
- **Input:** $R$ (Correctly Understood Intent) + $C$ (Authority, Budget, Physics)
- **Output:** $M$ (Manifested Action)
* *Failure Mode:* **"Understanding is not the same as being able to act."** The receiver understands perfectly ($R$ is correct), but structure ($C$) blocks the output ($M \to 0$).

---
**Architect's Note:**
Most management failures occur because leaders try to fix Step 1 (by over-explaining $S$), while the blockage is actually at Step 2 (Structural Constraint).

## 7. Why UI Stops Working

Judgment Structure began as an exploration of how UI might support decision-making.

Through observation, a clear boundary emerged.

### 7.1 What UI Can Do

UI can support judgment **before constraint dominates**.

Specifically, UI can help with:

- Surfacing missing references  
- Making comparison axes explicit  
- Aligning interpretation between Speaker ($S$) and Receiver ($R$)

This corresponds to cases where meaning failure is primarily caused by
misalignment or lack of reference.

Examples:
- **Type A — Reference Missing**
- **Type B — Reference-Resolved**

In these cases, UI intervention reduces cognitive load
and enables judgment to form.

---

### 7.2 What UI Cannot Do

UI cannot resolve judgment failures caused by dominant constraints.

UI cannot change:

- Authority and hierarchy
- Evaluation pressure
- Fear of responsibility
- Organizational power dynamics
- Physical or structural limits (time, budget, safety)

Once **Context / Constraint ($C$)** dominates meaning formation,
judgment failure is no longer a UI problem.

Examples:
- **Type D — Structural Mismatch**
- **Type E — Authority Override**
- **Type F — Explanation Avoidance**
- **Type G — Surface Compliance**

---

### 7.3 The Boundary Is Structural

This boundary is often misinterpreted as a design failure.

It is not.

UI stops working not because it is poorly designed,
but because meaning formation has moved outside the UI’s reach.

Judgment Structure exists to make this boundary visible,
not to erase it.

---

## 8. Why This Is Not About Solutions

This project does not propose fixes. It aims to:
- **Prevent misattribution:** Stopping the "just vague wording" excuse.
- **Prevent false optimism:** Stopping the "better UI will solve it" illusion.
- **Clarify boundaries:** Identifying where intervention is structurally impossible.

## 9. Implications for AI Alignment Research (Appendix)

This section does not propose solutions.

It reframes observed judgment failures in AI deployment
through the same structural lens used in this project.

---

### 9.1 Refusal as Structural Dominance ($w_c \to 1$)

When an AI system refuses a request due to safety policies,
this is not a failure of understanding.

It is a state where **constraint dominates meaning formation**.

In this condition:
- The user’s intent ($S$) is understood
- The model’s capability ($R$) is sufficient
- But the system constraint ($C$) overrides both

Structurally, this is equivalent to  
**Type E — Authority Override** in human organizations.

No amount of prompt optimization can fix this.
Only a change in constraint weight ($w_c$) can.

---

### 9.2 Hallucination as Reference Mismatch ($S \neq R$)

In creative or visual tasks, hallucination often arises from
a mismatch between:

- The user’s implicit context ($S$)
- The model’s statistical average ($R$)

Even if the model is statistically consistent with training distribution,
it can still be semantically misaligned with the user’s context.

This mirrors **Type A / B** failures:
- Missing references
- Unaligned comparison axes

The issue is not intelligence,
but lack of shared reference.

---

### 9.3 RLHF and Feedback Distortion

Human feedback used in alignment processes
is itself subject to structural constraint ($C$).

Raters may operate under:
- Time pressure
- Payment incentives
- Fear of negative evaluation
- Instructional authority

In such cases, feedback reflects **compliance**, not judgment.

This aligns with:
- **Type F — Explanation Avoidance**
- **Type G — Surface Compliance**

If feedback is structurally distorted,
models are aligned to surface-level agreement,
not to meaningful intent.

---

### 9.4 The Alignment Gap

Most alignment work focuses on improving:
- $S$ (instructions)
- $R$ (model capability)

Judgment Structure highlights a different failure zone:
**the gap between model output and real-world action**.

Meaning often collapses
not inside the model,
but when it enters human hierarchy and constraint.

This is the last mile problem of alignment.

## Appendix: A Philosophical Parallel (Optional)

This model closely mirrors the Buddhist concept of *Dependent Origination* (縁起),
which states that phenomena do not possess inherent meaning,
but arise from the combination of causes and conditions.

| Element | SRCM Definition | Dependent Origination |
|------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Word | A trigger without inherent meaning | 空 (emptiness) |
| S, R, C | Speaker, Receiver, Constraint | 因・縁 (causes & conditions) |
| M | Generated meaning | 果 (result) |

This is not a philosophical claim,
but an observation that similar structural insights
have emerged across different domains.
